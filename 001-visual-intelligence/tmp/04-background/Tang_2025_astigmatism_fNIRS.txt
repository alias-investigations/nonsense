The primary objective of this research is to explore changes in brain activity among astigmatism
patients, employing functional near-infrared spectroscopy (fNIRS). A prospective cross-sectional study
comprising 57 participants (99 eyes) was conducted and segmented into three distinct sections. In
PART I, 24 participants (48 eyes) with astigmatism (Group A) and 18 participants (36 eyes) without
astigmatism (Group B) were assessed using fNIRS to investigate the variations across different
brain regions and channels. PART II involved 15 participants with monocular astigmatism (15 eyes),
who received complete spherical optical correction, followed by fNIRS assessments after visual
stimulation using E-Prime software. The study examined the activation levels of the occipital visual
cortex and the functional connectivity strength of the brain, comparing these metrics pre- and post-
cylindrical correction. PART III: Following the correction of the initial refractive error in Group A, + 2.00
diopter cylinder (DC) lenses were employed to induce astigmatism at various axes. These axes were
categorized into three groups: oblique astigmatism group, with-the-rule astigmatism (WTR), and
against-the-rule astigmatism (ATR). fNIRS was conducted during task performance to assess the
strength and activation level of brain functional connectivity across different astigmatism axes of the
same diopter. PART I: The functional connectivity strength of HbO -based occipital cortex was stronger
2
in group B than in group A (P < 0.001). PART II: Significant differences in the β values of the left and
right visual cortex were detected in cases of monocular astigmatism before and after astigmatism
correction. (P = 0.002 and P = 0.004). The functional connectivity strength in the post-cylindrical
correction condition exceeded that in the pre-cylindrical correction condition; nevertheless, this
variation did not attain statistical significance (P > 0.05). PART III: The functional connectivity strength
and activation level of the HbO -based occipital visual cortex exhibited significant variations across
2
different astigmatism axes (P < 0.001 and P = 0.045). Specifically, the functional connectivity strength
in the oblique astigmatism group was greater than that observed in both the WTR and ATR groups.
Conversely, the degree of brain activation in the oblique astigmatism group was lower compared to the
ATR group (P = 0.042). These observations imply that astigmatism may decrease both the activation of
the occipital visual cortex and the strength of brain functional connectivity. The functional activities of
the occipital cortex can be enhanced to a certain degree following optical correction of astigmatism.
The strength of functional connectivity and the degree of activation in the occipital visual cortex vary
with different axes of astigmatism.

Astigmatic errors differ from myopic and hyperopic ones, because differential ocular surface curvature causes
inability to focus image points as single points on the retina; this results in blurred vision at any distance1.
Approximately 20–40% of the population have at least 1 diopter (D) of refractive astigmatism2. However, when
lower magnitudes of astigmatism are included, the prevalence can exceed 60%3. Research indicates that the
prevalence of astigmatism rises with age. The prevalence of astigmatism > 1.00 DC is approximately 8.30–11.60%
in individuals aged 5–30 years, 15.70–21.00% in those aged 30–60 years, and 31.10–81.80% in those aged
60–90 years4. Patients with astigmatism can adapt to projected minimal diffuse spots onto the retina, thereby
enhancing unaided vision. However, sustained accommodation may lead to increased fatigue, and uncorrected
astigmatism can adversely affect visual quality5. Despite the correction of astigmatism, visual acuity (VA) remains
significantly diminished in certain meridian directions. Even when employing laser interferometry, which
bypasses the optical system of the eye to project sinusoidal fringes directly onto the retina, the measured retinal
VA continues to exhibit variability across different meridians. This phenomenon suggests that the underlying
mechanism may involve the visual cortex6. Clinical evidence dating back to 1969 indicates that astigmatism
can be present at birth in infants and young children, and when astigmatism exceeds 0.75 DC, it can cause
symptoms such as blurred vision and diplopia7. In terms of animal model studies, myopic astigmatism alters
the electrophysiological responses of the retina in chickens, particularly delaying the inner retinal responses
in the central region. The influence of astigmatism on retinal responses exhibits region-specific characteristics
and correlates with the axis of astigmatism8, and astigmatism is also a major risk factor for amblyopia9. One
year following cylindrical intervention for astigmatism in school-aged children, their visual function (mainly
spherical aberration) remains inferior to that of children without astigmatism10. Furthermore, a reduction in
the amplitude of visual electrophysiological responses is evident in children with astigmatism11. This suggests
that visual performance is influenced by both the ocular optical system and the response of the central visual
nervous system12. Consequently, optical correction of astigmatism alone may not suffice to achieve optimal
visual outcomes, potentially implicating the developmental interplay of the brain’s central visual pathways.
Prior research has demonstrated that refractive errors significantly impact the functional activity of the
visual cortex. Visual electrophysiological investigations have indicated that myopic defocus can result in a
reduction of the P100 amplitude and an extension of its latency13–15. Functional magnetic resonance imaging
(fMRI) and fNIRS provide direct evidence of alterations in hemodynamic responses, as well as changes in
brain structure and function, in individuals with high myopia16,17. Freeman et al.18 discovered that individuals
with astigmatism exhibited differences in resolution between the vertical and horizontal meridians, even after
refractive correction.; Numerous subsequent studies have documented that individual with high astigmatism
exhibit direction-specific visual deficits even following optical correction after the critical period of visual
development10,19. During this critical period, abnormal visual input resulting from uncorrected astigmatism
can adversely impact the development of spatial vision, potentially leading to the onset of meridian amblyopia20.
Furthermore, neurological deficits in the V1 region of the visual cortex have been observed in both animal
experiments and human psychophysical studies investigating meridian amblyopia induced by astigmatism21.
Research involving animal models has shown that early abnormalities in visual input can significantly impact
the neural tissue within the mammalian visual system, with the effects of such exposure being permanent
and irreversible even after the restoration of normal visual input6,12. The majority of neurokines influencing
binocular visual development are located in the V1 region, where neural connections commence formation
shortly after birth. The maturation of visual processing and neural connections is contingent upon early visual
experiences. Prolonged exposure to distorted visual input during critical periods of visual development may
adversely affect normal visual processing and the development of neural connections22. Specifically, abnormal
visual experiences resulting from astigmatism can disrupt the development of functional connectivity in
the visual cortex, which subsequently manifests in perceptual behavior during adulthood. In comparison to
individuals without astigmatism, adults with astigmatism exhibit a significant distortion in shape perception,
which is only partially rectified through optical correction aimed at improving distorted visual input23. These
discoveries suggest a connection between astigmatism and the nervous system, implicating the visual cortex
as a key component in the underlying mechanism. However, current research on the mechanisms underlying
astigmatism predominantly focuses on the optical system24–26, with insufficient systematic investigation into its
effects on central visual function.
fNIRS is a non-invasive brain imaging technique that utilizes the unique absorption properties of near-
infrared light (600–900 nm wavelength) by hemoglobin in cerebral tissue, enabling real-time detection of blood
oxygenation dynamics in the cerebral cortex27. Due to its non-invasive nature and capability for continuous
monitoring, this approach provides an innovative and straightforward method for analyzing, diagnosing,
and treating diseases in clinical practice. Studies have found that fNIRS demonstrates good sensitivity and
repeatability in detecting cognitive load and states, suggesting that fNIRS is well-suited for exploring the full
complement of neuroimaging research questions and can serve as a viable alternative to fMRI28,29. At present,
it is predominantly applied in brain diseases, psychiatric problems, cognitive fields and sports rehabilitation,
but there are few related studies in the field of ophthalmic diseases, with only a few reports on strabismus
amblyopia30,31, diabetic retinopathy32, pathological myopia33, etc. fNIRS serves as a valuable complement to
existing modalities such as fMRI, positron emission tomography (PET), and electroencephalography (EEG).
The near-infrared light emitted by fNIRS can penetrate the skull and reach the cerebral cortex to a depth of
approximately 2–3 cm. Within the cortex, the light undergoes scattering and propagation before exiting the
skull, where it is detected by a collection device. The intensity of the collected near-infrared light varies with the
concentration of blood oxygen in the cerebral cortex. An increase in neural activity within a specific brain region
leads to a corresponding rise in cerebral blood flow, which is reflected by alterations in hemoglobin levels. This
relationship between cortical activation and blood flow is referred to as neurovascular coupling34. In comparison
to fMRI, which relies on magnetic fields, fNIRS detects alterations in hemoglobin concentration through optical

principles. It offers superior temporal resolution and is more accessible for a broader demographic due to its
user-friendly operation and portability. When contrasted with PET, fNIRS is non-invasive, posing fewer risks
to the human body, and allows for repeated measurements. Additionally, compared to EEG, fNIRS provides
enhanced spatial resolution, facilitating a more precise correlation between anatomical structures and brain
function.
Given the limited research on alterations in brain function within the visual cortex of individuals with
astigmatism, this investigation employed fNIRS to examine differences in the strength of brain connectivity
between individuals with and without astigmatism. Additionally, the research aimed to identify changes in brain
function before and after optical correction in participants with moderate and low astigmatism, and to assess
potential differences in activation levels between the left and right visual cortex in individuals with monocular
astigmatism. An additional aim of this investigation is to examine the variations in alterations of brain function
associated with different axes of astigmatism, with the aim of elucidating the central mechanisms underlying
astigmatism and generating insights for enhancing the visual quality of individuals affected by this condition.
Patients and methods
Patients
A cohort of 60 healthy young individuals who underwent routine ophthalmic examinations at the outpatient
clinic of the Affiliated Hospital of North Sichuan Medical College between May 2023 and October 2023 was
initially selected for this study. However, one participant was unable to complete the entire experimental
procedure, and two were excluded due to poor signal acquisition quality. Thus, the final sample consisted of 57
participants. Among these, 39 presented with astigmatism, including 15 individuals with monocular astigmatism
(15 eyes) and 24 individuals with binocular astigmatism (48 eyes). The remaining 18 participants had no
astigmatism (36 eyes) and served as the control group. Astigmatism was determined using an autorefractor
and subjective refraction made with a phoropter. Meanwhile, astigmatism correction was performed using trial
frame spectacles. For Part Ⅲ, based on the correction of each participant’s original refractive error, + 2.00 D
cylindrical lenses in a trial frame were used to induce astigmatism of the same diopter but with different axes.
Inclusion criteria: (1) aged 18–30 years, with best corrected VA of 0.0 (Log MAR) in both eyes; (2) axial
length < 26 mm; (3) spherical power > −6.00 DS; (4) cylindrical power between −0.50 DC and −2.00 DC (for the
astigmatism groups); (5) cylindrical power less than 0.50 DC (for the non-astigmatic group).Exclusion criteria:
(1) Eye diseases such as pathological myopia, glaucoma, and corneal disease that affect vision and visual quality;
(2) history of any ocular surgery or trauma; (3) Those who cannot cooperate with the inspection process.
The requisite sample size for this investigation was ascertained utilizing PASS 15.0 software, with a significance
threshold defined at α = 0.05 and a test power (1-β) of 0.9. Based on the estimated sample rate for comparing
the means of a t-test between two independent samples, the total sample consisted of 32 cases. According to
the sample rate estimation of the paired sample t-test mean comparison, the total sample size was 11 cases.
Based on the sample rate estimate of one-way analysis of variance (ANOVA) mean comparison, the total sample
comprised 30 cases. The overall sample size for this study comprised 57 cases, thereby providing a statistically
significant basis for analysis.
fNIRS
Previous studies have demonstrated that fNIRS has excellent reproducibility, reliability and validity in the
assessment of brain function35–37. The variation in hemoglobin concentration within the visual cortex of the
occipital lobe was continuously monitored using a near-infrared spectroscopy based functional brain imaging
apparatus. (Danyang Huichuang Medical Equipment Co., Ltd., Jiangsu, China). The system is comprised of
a near-infrared light source, specifically a light-emitting diode (LED), and an avalanche photodiode (APD)
functioning as detectors. The light source probes operate at wavelengths of 760 nm and 850 nm, individually,
with a sampling rate of 11 Hz. In the experimental setup, four light source probes and four detectors were utilized,
resulting in eight effective channels. Channels CH01 to CH04 were designated for the right occipital lobe, while
channels CH05 to CH08 were allocated to the left occipital lobe. The distance between the transmitter and the
detector varies from 2.7 to 3.3 cm, with both components secured using a black head cap. HbO is the most
2
sensitive marker for changes in local cerebral blood flow and is therefore the most effective marker for reflecting
changes in brain function. Consequently, this study primarily focuses on the analysis of HbO variations.

Data collection
During the resting state data collection, the specialized examination room is maintained in a quiet environment.
The participant is seated comfortably in a chair and fitted with a black head cap, which is arranged following the
International 10–20 system. The central electrode is aligned with the occipital protuberance, and the remaining
electrodes are securely attached to the scalp. The duration of the test is 8 min, during which the participant is
instructed to refrain from speaking and to minimize head and body movements. During the task state data
collection, the experimental conditions mirrored those of the head-mounted apparatus. Each measurement was
performed once. The time interval between correction or induced astigmatism and experimental data collection
was similar across all participants. Visual stimulation tasks were administered and data were recorded using
E-Prime 3.0 software. Resting-state data acquisition was employed in Part I, whereas task-based data acquisition
was adopted in Parts II and III. The flowchart of the methodology is shown in Fig. 1.

a resolution of 1920 × 1080 pixels. As illustrated in Fig. 2, the overall duration of stimulation was 140 s. During

Data processing and statistical analysis
The initial spectral data collected were preprocessed using NirSpark software. Temporal regions not pertinent
to the experiment were excluded, and motion artifacts and filtering were removed. Subsequently, changes in
blood oxygen concentration were calculated in accordance with the modified Beer-Lambert law. The functional
connectivity strength and β value for each channel were analyzed, with the β value employed to assess the
activation degree of the visual cortex. Statistical analyses were carried out with SPSS software, version 26.0. The
Shapiro–Wilk test utilized to evaluate the conformity of the data to a normal distribution. Data conforming
to a normal distribution were expressed as the mean ± standard deviation (x±s), while data deviating from
a normal distribution were represented by the median and interquartile range [M (Q1, Q3)]. Disparities in
age, intraocular pressure, and gender across the two groups were evaluated employing independent samples
t-tests and chi-square tests. Specifically, the independent samples t-test was utilized to evaluate differences in
the strength of brain functional connectivity between groups A and B. In the second section of the analysis, a
paired sample t-test was employed to evaluate the differences in brain functional connectivity strength and β
values between groups C and D, as well as to assess the disparities in β values between the left and right occipital
visual cortices. Subsequently, in the third section, a repeated measures ANOVA was conducted to examine
the variations in brain functional connectivity strength and β values across different axial groups within the
same diopter. The Bonferroni method was utilized for pairwise comparisons. A p-value below 0.05 was deemed
indicative of statistical significance.

Discussion

connectivity associated with astigmatism. It compares alterations in visual cortex brain function before and
after astigmatism correction, as well as between the left and right hemispheres in subjects with monocular
astigmatism. Additionally, it examines the changes in visual cortex brain function induced by lenses with
varying astigmatism axes oriented downward. Gao et al.39 investigated the impact of astigmatism on visual
quality in children using the OPD-SCAN III aberrometer. Their results showed that as the degree of astigmatism
increased, the root mean square values of total higher-order aberrations, total coma, total spherical aberration,

Consistent with the animal literature highlighting the vulnerability of the visual cortex to abnormal early
visual input6,12, the present study provides neuroimaging evidence in humans that astigmatism is associated
with cortical functional changes. While previous research has primarily focused on the optical components of
astigmatism39, our fNIRS findings suggest that the neural impact extends to the visual cortex, particularly in
adults with a history of prolonged distorted visual input during development. The persistent shape perception
biases observed behaviorally in adults with astigmatism, even after optical correction23, imply a neural rather
than purely optical origin. Our results offer a potential neural correlate for this phenomenon: the aberrant
cortical activation patterns detected by fNIRS may reflect the long-term adaptation of the visual system to
chronic asymmetric or degraded input. It is plausible that these cortical changes represent the neural substrate
underlying the incomplete recovery of perceptual function following correction. Although the current study
did not directly measure behavioral performance, the observed cortical changes in adults with astigmatism may
provide insight into the neural basis of these persistent perceptual deficits. Future investigations integrating
fNIRS with concurrent behavioral assessments (e.g., contrast sensitivity, orientation discrimination, or shape
perception tasks) are warranted to establish direct correlations between neural activation patterns and visual
performance, thereby elucidating the precise relationship between cortical function and perceptual behavior in
astigmatism.

Astigmatism, being a vector quantity, influences visual quality not solely based on its magnitude but also in
relation to its axis orientation. Mimouni et al. 41conducted an evaluation of the impact of axial astigmatism on
VA in patients exhibiting residual astigmatism following refractive surgery. Their findings indicated that the
influence of ondated astigmatism was less pronounced compared to that of retrograde astigmatism and oblique
axial astigmatism. The influence of the astigmatism axis seems to be associated with the "tilt effect," wherein
visual stimuli exhibit greater resolving power along the horizontal and vertical axes compared to the oblique
axis42. This phenomenon has been corroborated by studies employing adaptive optics12 and fMRI43, which
suggest a connection to neural mechanisms within the visual cortex. Furthermore, it is hypothesized that this
effect may be linked to the directional sensitivity of retinal ganglion cells8. Astigmatism exhibits a neuroadaptive
phenomenon whereby the human visual system, when subjected to persistent astigmatism and defocus, can
adapt to the resultant blurring. This adaptation leads to enhancements in both VA and visual quality2. Clinically,
this phenomenon has been observed to exhibit selectivity concerning the axis of astigmatism, with oblique axis
astigmatism proving more challenging to adapt to compared to compliant and retrograde astigmatism. Therefore,
the impact of the axial direction of astigmatism was further examined across different groups in our study. In
examining the strength of brain functional connectivity associated with the occipital visual cortex of the occipital
lobe during task-state HbO brain activity, statistically significant differences were observed among patients

in the oblique astigmatism group was greater than that observed in both the oblique astigmatism group and
the antigrade astigmatism group., The findings suggest that the strength of functional brain connectivity varies
with different astigmatic axial stimulations. This variation may be attributed to the blurring effects induced by
lens-induced oblique axis astigmatism, which poses greater adaptive challenges compared to the other two axes.
Oblique axis astigmatism demands heightened attention and visual processing, necessitating the recruitment of
a broader array of brain regions to effectively integrate visual feedback. Abnormal visual experiences resulting
from astigmatism can disrupt brain development, leaving “traces” in the adult visual cortex. This phenomenon
may be associated with the diminished collinear facilitation effect observed in oblique axis astigmatism, which
arises from interactions among optic neurons and can be regarded as an intermediate stage in the processing of

exhibited a lower degree of brain activation compared to the ATR group. These results suggest that the activation
level of the visual cortex varies according to the axis of astigmatism. Neurodeficits in the V1 region of the
visual cortex have been documented in both animal experiments and human psychophysical studies concerning
astigmatism-induced meridian amblyopia21. The primary visual cortex cells exhibit orientation selectivity, with
neurons in the V1 region demonstrating a predisposition towards horizontal and vertical orientations over
inclined ones45. For example, Gu et al.20 employed a combination of psychophysical and electroencephalogram
methodologies to investigate contrast sensitivity and visual evoked potentials along the two principal meridians
in children without astigmatism and those with meridian amblyopia. Their study confirmed the horizontal and
vertical asymmetry associated with human astigmatism in spatial vision and identified direction-specific visual
developmental deficits in the primary visual cortex. Additionally, Shiba et al.46 conducted a study involving
refractive correction on two subjects, followed by the administration of + 6.00 diopters of compliant and
retrograde astigmatism defocus. fMRI was utilized to assess the activation levels of the visual cortex during
visual stimulation. The findings indicated that astigmatism potentially influences the human visual cortex, with
variations in activation levels observed across different axes.
However, this study has some limitations. Primarily, the investigation was confined to the occipital visual
cortex, leaving the involvement of functional changes in other brain regions undetermined. The exclusive use of
fNIRS, while providing valuable hemodynamic data, limits our ability to fully characterize neural mechanisms.
The absence of complementary techniques like EEG (for temporal resolution) or fMRI (for spatial precision)
means we cannot determine whether observed hemodynamic changes reflect altered neural timing or specific
subcortical contributions. Although we included measures of VA (Log MAR) and contrast sensitivity (Pelli-
Robson), our study lacked behavioral assessments related to visual function, such as tests of visual perception,
dynamic acuity, or real-world functional vision. Additionally, the study’s broader development was constrained
by the operational complexity of the testing equipment and the high level of participant cooperation required.
While we reported functional connectivity differences between astigmatism subgroups, the absence of repeated
fNIRS measurements in the same subjects prevented us from performing additional validation analyses such as
test–retest reliability assessment. And the relatively small sample size in certain subgroups, which could reduce
the robustness of signal quality comparisons. Furthermore, this study included individuals with monocular
astigmatism and those with binocular astigmatism. However, a direct comparison between these groups was
not feasible due to the experimental design. The monocular astigmatism group was assessed using a within-
subject design (comparing cortical activation before and after correction under monocular viewing), whereas
the binocular astigmatism group was assessed using a between-subject design (comparing with healthy controls
under binocular viewing). Directly comparing these two groups would confound the effects of astigmatism type
with differences in viewing conditions. Future studies employing a unified protocol are needed to systematically
compare these two refractive conditions. This remains a limitation of the present work. In addition, the sample
selection was relatively limited, as participants with a broader range of spherical refractive errors combined with
astigmatism were not included for further analysis.
Future research should pursue three key directions to advance understanding of astigmatism-related
cerebral functional changes. First, expanded sample sizes with balanced representation across astigmatism
subtypes are needed to clarify their differential neural effects. Second, multimodal integration of fNIRS with
fMRI technology will enable simultaneous, validated assessment of visual pathway activation patterns with
complementary spatial and temporal resolution. Finally, establishing standardized protocols specifically for
astigmatic populations—including rigorous test–retest reliability assessments, refractive-error-adapted signal
quality metrics, and individualized optode placement strategies—will be critical for enhancing methodological
rigor. These coordinated advances will substantially improve both the reproducibility and clinical applicability
of neuroimaging findings in visual impairment research, ultimately facilitating more precise characterization
of how different astigmatism subtypes influence brain function. This approach will facilitate the acquisition of
more comprehensive and reliable data, thereby providing a deeper understanding of the changes and patterns in
brain function among patients with clinical astigmatism.
Conclusion
The present findings suggest that uncorrected astigmatism may lead to reduced activation in the occipital
visual cortex and weakened brain functional connectivity. Notably, optical correction of astigmatism appears to
enhance the functional activity of the occipital cortex, at least partially, highlighting the potential neuroplastic
effects of vision restoration. Furthermore, the degree of cortical activation and the strength of functional
connectivity vary depending on the axis of astigmatism, implying that the neural impact of astigmatism may be
orientation-dependent. However, these connectivity differences were derived from fNIRS measurements, and
further research is needed to validate the sensitivity and reliability of this neuroimaging modality in populations
